AI Localism

How are cities performing AI Localism?

Examining Use Cases from across the AI Localism Canvas

Rapid urbanization and expansion has paved the way for increased technological innovation in cities. While nations and supranational blocs debate the limits of technology use at a federal level, increasingly, cities are leading the charge by implementing policies at a quicker, more direct, and impactful level.

The need to recognise state/city level governance, law, and policy

More and more, state- and city-wide legislation are advancing progressive policy agendas and focusing on the lack of legal effectiveness that national legislatures fall victim to. For example, in May 2019, San Francisco became the first U.S. city to ban police and local government agencies from using facial recognition technology to surveill and identify residents, addressing a growing threat to Fourth Amendment protections.

Numerous types of ‘localism’ exist to fill policy gaps in communities overlooked by national governments. Instances of broadband localism can be found in Sandy, Oregon, wherein the local government created ‘SandyNet’ to provide high-speed DSL and wireless internet connection at low costs. Examples of privacy localism include New York City’s local ordinance to regulate collection and use of citizen data by governments and law enforcement agencies. Further, in a move towards data sovereignty, some cities are practicing data localism, or requiring data collected by companies about residents to be stored on local servers.

We call instances of AI and data use and collection regulation as ‘AI localism.’ AI localism refers to the actions taken by local decision-makers to address the use of AI within a city. Often, these policies emerge because of gaps in higher-level governance frameworks and/or because of the proliferation of certain types of technology, such as facial recognition, within a community.

It is necessary to note however, that the presence of AI Localism does not mean that the call for robust national and state-level legislature must be slowed down, but rather such local level governance must complement and fuel overall ethical and inclusive AI governance.

To this end, the AI localism project crafted a taxonomy on the dimensions of regulation (see below). This series presents current examples of AI localism across the canvas frames:

  • Principles and Rights: foundational requirements and constraints of AI and algorithmic use in the public sector;
  • Literacy: avenues to educate policymakers and the public about AI and data;
  • Transparency: consumer awareness about AI and algorithm use;
  • Accountability and Oversight: requirements for periodic reporting and auditing of AI use;
  • Engagement: public involvement in AI use and limitations;
  • Procurement: mandates around the use of AI in employment and hiring practices; and
  • Laws and Policies: regulation to codify the above for public and private sectors
